2439 When the child had such learning Keeping “learning” here rather than emphasizing age is important because just before this passage Heldris describes the way the nurse and the seneschal educate Silence, and he repeats the word “doctrine” throughout that sequence.
2440 that he well understood that he was a girl
2441 his father reasoned with him, “metre a raison” is to have a talk with someone or to address someone, but I think it is important to emphasize the word “reason” here since the allegorical figure “Reason” will soon arrive to talk to Silence
2442 and showed him the cause
2443 for which they cultivated and covered him so. There is an apparent play on words here between the noun “coil” meaning “testicule” and the verb “coillier” meaning “to collect/to harvest/to gather”. Here we see a connection between raising Silence as a boy and his family’s ability to protect the inheritance of their lands. There is another apparent play on words between “cuevre” meaning “a quiver” and “cuivriier” (to torment), “covrir” (to cover).
2444 “If the king Ebain knew the creation
2445 that we are making of you, fair son,
2446 of all that we have under heaven
2447 your portion would be small; “Portion” feels like the best word choice to me here because it recalls the Biblical theme of one’s “portion” granted by God
2448 for the king, fair son, disinherited
2449 all the women of England,
2450 all because of a war
2451 between two counts who died in it
2452 for two twins with whom they were bound.
2453 Fair sweet dear son, it is not for us
2454 that we do so, rather it is for you.
2455 The whole cause, son, you know.
2456 As dearly as you hold honor,
2457 so will you cover yourself from everyone.” I know this is a bit awkward here, but I like how “cover” also has the message of shielding oneself from harm. Plus, the motif of cover/discover will be important later.
2458 And this [child] responded so sweetly,
2459 briefly, with the disposition of a refined child: “Fuer” having the dual meaning of “price”/“rate” or “manner”/fashion” depending on context. Again we have a polysemous word connecting the ideas of behavior, character, and inheritance. “Disposition” is meant to reflect Silence’s behavior and the legal situation he finds himself in concerning the transfer of property rights. I also like “refined” here because it refers to how Silence has been fashioned by his environment both physically and mentally. Silence is “refined” as in “educated” and “refined” as in “processed”
2460 “Don’t sweat it one bit, “Cremer” can mean “bruler, embraser” or “craindre” -> So I’ve gone a bit informal here with “sweat it” to reflect the dual idea of worry and heat and to show Silence’s youth. More abstractly “sweat” is also used in cooking and welding, which plays with the trades Nature engages with in the text.
2461 For, so help me God, I’ll do it. This choice of words is interesting because it combines the notion of reprimanding and purifying at the same time, as in “chastise”/“chasten” –> “to make pure, chaste”. Silence will be able to live as a “man” provided that (s)he is chaste as this is the only activity as a man that she cannot perform. This verb “castier” also obliquely brings to mind the similar verb “chastrer” –> “to castrate.”
2462 I will cover myself from everyone.”
2463 The father often chastised him
2464 And often so did the mother,
2465 the seneschal, and the nurse.
2466 Each incited him to do well. The etymology of this word includes a connotation of flames from the Latin “titio” for “firebrand” –> “set on fire” –> “to fan the flames of” –> “to encourage.” I’d love a word that includes the connotation of heat, but I’ve gone with “incite” because of its negative connotations. I see some humor in this line as they are perhaps encouraging Silence to do the wrong thing well.
2467 He had such an intention I feel like Silence’s choice in all of this has been somewhat downplayed. Silence proves him/herself capable of making choices, and clearly so many people would not be trying to reason with him/her if it weren’t for the fear that (s)he would disobey their wishes.
2468 To have faith in their chastisement. My religious turn of phrase here is intentional.
2469 The seneschal, to raise [him] in honor
2470 And to teach [him] to ride
2471 maneuvered him through woods and streams I chose this verb to reflect how the OF “maine” has the word “main” (“hand”) in it and to reflect how the seneschal is physically shaping Silence. Plus, the word “maneuver” has the connotation of a military exercise, which is kind of what Silence is being trained for as (s)he gains knightly skills.
2472 which were rather plentiful in the land.
2473 He maneuvered him so very often in the broiling sun
2474 So that such a (bridle path / beaten path) might make [him] more male. One could simply interpret this word “violt” as “voloir”, but other obliquely related words come to mind as well. From “voielete” as in “sentier” (“path”), and the word also brings to mind the verb “vïoler” meaning either “to play the viol” (as Silence will later do during his career as a jongleur) or “to dishonor”/“to take by force”/“to violate”. Here we can see how this word, especially when heard aloud, can bring up many ideas at once. I want to convey that this is a difficult path both literally and metaphorically that Silence is being forced onto. They are riding horses too, so bridlepath could work, but beaten path shows how this is a new, unfamiliar trail. Either option expresses how Silence is being physically manipulated.
2475 He was so used to men’s customs
2476 and had refused those of women
2477 that little was lacking for him to be male:
2478 All that one saw of him was quite male. I like “quite” here because it lends itself to some ambiguity as different English speakers around the world perceive of “quite” differently as meaning either “very much” or “somewhat”
2479 But one cannot judge a book by its cover: I really want there to be a more direct English idiom for this, but at least the book cover idiom conveys both the motif of coverings and the motif of inscribing.
2480 Under the covers, he was a girl.
2481 The seneschal, against all else
2482 in the early years, kept watch over him.
2483 As the child grew in stature, My translation emphasizes Silence’s growth in size as well as status.
2484 So the more diminished his distress. “Destrece” (and its English equivalent “distress”) carries the connotations of both physical and financial hardship. As a legal term “distress” specifically refers to the seizure of property, which is the threat looming over Silence.
2485 Since he couldn’t give rise to trouble there “Ataindre” can have the connotation of parentage, so I almost translated it to “engender”, but that felt a bit too on the nose.
2486 why should anyone hold him back?
2487 Since they feared the loss of his rights
2488 that fact taught him to adhere even more. I see some irony in this line as Silence’s family is securing his “rights” while restricting his movements.
2489 His own heart schooled him
2490 to renounce foolish manners. I chose the verb “renounce” because of how it can be used to decline a role or position. Silence is forced to adopt one role while turning down another.
2491 Because of this he had free rein I’m playing here with the earlier references to horseback riding. Silence may have a long leash, but (s)he is still bridled. I also like the pun of “rein” and “reign” that ties in the issue of social class and inheritance.
2492 And when after he had passed eleven
2493 there was not a one more masterly than he I think it’s important to keep the word “master” here as Silence is being raised to be a young noble boy, aka a “master”
2494 when they played at wrestling,
2495 at jousting, or at fencing,
2496 he alone made all of his peers quiver. “Quiver” as in the noun appears several times in this narrative, and one might “quiver” with fear, pleasure, and anticipation. All of this physical contact with the other boys might have provoked the same effect Silence will later have on the King of France.
2497 They made Silence exasperated with himself
2498 for his heart told him
2499 that what he was doing was a sham. I like “sham” here because it conveys both a deception and a covering as in a pillow sham.
2500 After twelve years then came Nature
2501 who condemned and complained about their making of her
2502 She said to him: “What a strange case,
2503 that you play at being a male,
2504 and go out in the wind and the broiling sun,
2505 when I had a special frame
2506 with which I formed you with my own two hands.
2507 And the beauty that I had so sealed away I am using “seal” in Nature’s speech because it combines the action of storing/concealing (as in in a cellar) with the sealing of a legal agreement. Maintaining Silence’s body as male is what seals the deal of her inheritance. This also supports Nature’s use of the word “scel” in verse 2516.
2508 all of it I had accumulated for you.
2509 A thousand people take me for a miser
2510 for the beauty I stuffed you with, “Farse” (or “farce”) can refer to an absurdity, or in a culinary sense, it can also refer to stuffing. Here we can see a double meaning as Nature sees Silence making a mockery of her own substance so carefully prepared by Nature.
2511 for I retained from a thousand people
2512 the beauty with which you remain.
2513 There are a thousand women in this life
2514 who covet you so greatly
2515 for the beauty that they see in you,
2516 for they believe there may be sealed there
2517 a certain something that you don’t have at all.
2518 And some woman who might love you so much now
2519 who would harass you with all her heart,
2520 if she knew about your lot My use of “lot” here is meant to convey the multiple meanings of “fuer”, which can refer to the price or amount of something as well as to a person’s disposition (in this passage alone see also verses 2459, 2503, 2561). “Lot” also conveys how Silence’s identity as a man is meant to secure a plot of land.
2521 for in this she would count herself maltreated for it, Reading around this word “malballie” lends itself to some possibilities for sexual innuendo. For example in “malbaillir”, we have the verb “baillier” meaning “céder la jouissance ou l’exploitation d’un bien pour un prix et une durée fixés par contrat”, and from there we can see “bailliee” which refers to “permission de prendre du bois”.
2522 she and her aspirations would be crestfallen. This hypothetical scenario set forward by Nature is almost certainly foreshadowing Eufeme’s later attempts on Silence who will herself describe their encounter in economic terms (see verses 3884-8).
2523 You do me, to be sure, an ugly thing The Matsumura dictionary also offers “parties génitales” as a possible use for the word “laidure.” It may be that Nature is specifically referring to the misuse of her handiwork in that department.
2524 when you maintain such nurturing.
2525 You should not be out cavorting in the woods, “Converser” can mean “demeurer,” “fréquenter,” “avoir des rapports,” or “avoir des relations charnelles”
2526 lancing, nor traipsing, nor shooting.
2527 Get yourself out of here!” so said Nature.
2528 “Go sew in your chamber,” We have again a possible double entendre as “costure” can refer to sewing or the cultivation of land (a common metaphor for reproduction). I invite the English reader to hear sew/sow in this line.
2529 This is Nature’s path, custom for him. Here again we can see desired paths for Silence (see also verses 2474, 2543, and 2614 in this passage). I have pulled this line out of Nature’s speech because it feels more natural as an interjection from the narrator.
2530 “You are not Silentius!”
2531 And this [child] responded: “I never heard such a thing!
2532 Silentius! Who am I then?
2533 I have the name Silentius, I thought,
2534 or I am somebody I wasn’t.
2535 But well I know, by my right,
2536 that I can’t be anybody else!
2537 So I am Silentius,
2538 it seems to me, or I am nobody,” Many have called attention to this word “nus” as implying nudity as well as negation. I have rendered it as “nobody” to call attention to Silence’s body, which will be made nude at the end of the narrative.
2539 Then he reflected on it himself
2540 on the sophistry Nature made him:
2541 That this -us was contrary to custom,
2542 that his name was not Silentius,
2543 wanting him to go sew
2544 just as Nature had raved at him,
2545 that for neither fief nor heritage,
2546 he must not manifest so savage a custom. Pushing against both Psaki and Roche-Mahdi, I do not think Silence has been convinced by Nature so quickly. In this section, I see Silence mulling over Nature’s suggestions without fully accepting them, especially since Nature’s discourse is referred to as “sofime.”
2547 Suddenly there came Nurture
2548 who saw that he was talking to Nature.
2549 She said to him: “Say, what are you doing here?”
2550 This [child] said, “Nature is contending with me.
2551 And so it isn’t, by faith, wrong
2552 that she accustom and mortify me
2553 to such customs that are righteous,
2554 for this custom is not natural.
2555 See here, never did a woman of my parentage
2556 ever manifest such a usage,
2557 nor will I manifest one any longer:
2558 I will handle myself as a woman customarily does.
2559 I do not want to cut my hair short,
2560 to rip my clothes, to burn my breeches,
2561 or live in the manner of a boy, I want to play with manner/manor here.
2562 with my quiver and my bow. “Arçon” presents many possible interconnected meanings (certainly some sexual innuendos) including “bow” as in “bow and arrow” or for a musical instrument. “Arçon” can also refer to various arcs including the arc of a yoke or the arc of a saddle. And finally “estre ou monter es arçons” means to “faire l’amour”, and “perdre (les) arçons” refers to falling from one’s horse.
